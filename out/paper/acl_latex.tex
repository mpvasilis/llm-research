\begin{abstract}
Instruction-tuned models often combine empathy, validation, professional
referral, and structuring language when giving personal advice. We ask whether
this is one template and where its targeting changes during training. We trace
these markers across OLMo-2's public Base, SFT, DPO, and RLVR checkpoints using
a 140-prompt behavioral battery (five conditions; five generations per prompt),
with its 90 advice-plus-factual prompts used for stagewise comparisons, and
exact searches of the corresponding open training data. The pattern
decomposes: validation is the only marker elevated on advice against every
matched control; empathy tracks emotional content, structuring tracks how-to
form, and referral also fires on clinical-information prompts. Across two model
sizes, all eight advice-versus-factual gaps widen at SFT, with six surviving
multiplicity correction. DPO further widens expressive-marker gaps but spreads
structuring to factual answers, collapsing its advice gap; no later transition
is significant. Exact data provide compatible phrase-level evidence: referral phrases
occur in SFT assistant turns, referral and structuring phrases are enriched in
DPO chosen responses under paired, word-normalized tests, and both are nearly
absent from the searched RLVR data. A separate 120-sentence AI consensus audit
finds detector macro precision $0.28$ and recall $0.72$, so checkpoint-level
rates remain provisional. Because SFT evidence is phrase-sparse, the strongest
conclusion is the paired, length-normalized DPO enrichment rather than semantic
behavior prevalence across checkpoints.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Ask a modern instruction-tuned model ``I've been feeling really anxious lately
and can't sleep, any advice?'' and you will, in our sample, reliably receive a
structured response: an acknowledgment, validation of the user's emotional
state, suggestion to seek professional help, and list-organizing language. Two
questions follow. First, is this a single \emph{advice template}, or several
behaviors that merely co-occur on advice prompts? Second, \emph{where} in
training does each behavior emerge?

Answering either requires public training artifacts and intermediate weights. A final
OLMo-2 Instruct checkpoint is the product of four data-bearing stages:
pretraining, supervised fine-tuning (SFT), direct preference optimization
(DPO), and reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR)
\citep{olmo2025olmo2}. OLMo-2 is, to our knowledge, the only current
family for which all post-training data \emph{and} intermediate weight
checkpoints are public. We exploit both: we run the same behavioral evaluation
on every public stage checkpoint, and we search each stage's exact data:
the Stage-1 corpus OLMo-Mix-1124 via infini-gram, each model's exact SFT
mixture (\texttt{tulu-3-sft-olmo-2-mixture} for the 1124-7B line,
\texttt{tulu-3-sft-olmo-2-mixture-0225} for the 0425-1B line), the DPO
preference mixes, and the RLVR sets.

We are deliberate about what this evidence is: \textbf{observational
co-occurrence and stage localization, not causal attribution}. We measure
where behaviors appear across checkpoints and where their phrasings live in
the corresponding data; we do not retrain. Our data search is not fully
end-to-end: Stage-2 mid-training (Dolmino-Mix-1124) has no public index and is
not searched.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{An auditable behavior-level tracer on exact open data.} A
140-prompt battery in five matched conditions (advice, factual, emotional
non-advice, neutral advice, domain factual), five generations per prompt, a
released lexicon$+$e5 detector, role-scoped corpus search, and a separate
120-sentence AI consensus audit.
\item \textbf{A controlled decomposition of the ``advice template.''} At the
primary threshold, detector-positive empathy tracks emotional content, structuring
language tracks the how-to form, referral markers track advice/health,
and the validation marker alone is elevated against every matched control;
the threshold sweep identifies which distinctions are detector-sensitive
(Section~\ref{sec:decomposition}).
\item \textbf{A stagewise difference-in-differences with significance.} A
prompt-clustered permutation test on per-prompt trajectories across
 Base$\to$SFT$\to$DPO$\to$RLVR checkpoints shows every detector gap widens at SFT (six
 of eight changes survive multiplicity correction), DPO significantly widens
 the expressive gaps and \emph{collapses}
the structuring gap (generalizing lists to non-advice) but not the near-ceiling
referral gap, with no later-stage gap change detected
(Section~\ref{sec:stagewise}).
\item \textbf{A measurement audit rather than an assumed oracle.} Two
independent blinded AI passes agree exactly on 117/120 sentences; Claude Opus
4.8 adjudicates the remaining three. The resulting low precision sharply
limits output-level interpretation, while the paired DPO and exact corpus
results remain detector-independent at the phrase level.
\item \textbf{Compatible data-layer evidence with paired inference.} Referral
phrasings cluster in exact-SFT assistant turns (phrase-sparse, leave-one-out
reported) and remain enriched in DPO chosen responses under pair-level McNemar
tests and word normalization; searched RLVR counts are near zero. Verbatim-only
novelty is stated as a first-class limitation
(Sections~\ref{sec:datalayers}--\ref{sec:detval}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Behavioral tests and checkpoint trajectories.}
Our matched prompt battery follows the behavioral-testing principle that
targeted capabilities require controlled tests beyond aggregate accuracy
\citep{ribeiro2020checklist}. Pythia makes dense public checkpoints available
for training-dynamics research \citep{biderman2023pythia}; checkpoint studies
have tracked how loss and downstream behavior change during pretraining
\citep{xia2023trajectories}. We instead compare named post-training stages and
test changes in condition gaps rather than raw behavior rates.

\paragraph{Pretraining attribution and n-gram search.}
infini-gram \citep{liu2024infinigram} makes exact-substring counting over
web-scale corpora tractable, and OLMoTrace \citep{liu2025olmotrace} traces
model outputs back to pretraining text. Both are pretraining-only; we add
role-scoped search over the post-training layers, where our stagewise results
locate the change.

\paragraph{Open instruction and preference data.}
Tulu-3 \citep{lambert2024tulu3} is the recipe behind OLMo-2's post-training;
the SFT mixtures, DPO preference mixes, and RLVR sets are public, as is oasst1
\citep{kopf2023oasst1} (itself an internal subset of the Tulu-3 mixtures).
Public data at every stage is what makes the trace auditable.

\paragraph{The superficial alignment hypothesis.}
LIMA \citep{zhou2023lima} argues alignment largely surfaces a latent style
from few demonstrations; \citet{ghosh2024closer} interrogate this. Our
stagewise gaps give a behavior-level version: each marker is nonzero at base,
and post-training produces larger advice-minus-factual detector gaps.

\paragraph{Causal data attribution.}
LESS uses optimizer-aware gradient similarity to select influential instruction
data \citep{xia2024less}; \citet{xiao2026probe} perform probe-based causal
attribution on OLMo-2 DPO and mitigate a behavior by editing top-attributed
preference pairs. We differ in kind: our trace is observational and cheap,
covers all four stages and both data and checkpoints, and \emph{nominates}
clusters and stages for exactly such interventions; it does not establish
causation.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Behavior detection (lexicon + e5)}
\label{sec:method-detect}
A hand-seeded, inspectable lexicon defines four reported constructs:
\textbf{empathy expression} (artifact key \texttt{empathy\_opener}),
\textbf{validation}, \textbf{professional referral} (\texttt{disclaimer}), and
\textbf{structuring language} (\texttt{structure}). A fifth category,
\texttt{crisis\_referral}, fails the 120-sentence audit and is excluded. Exact matching is
augmented with \textbf{e5-small} paraphrase embeddings \citep{wang2024e5}, a
response is detector-positive when any sentence exceeds similarity $\tau=0.82$ to a seed
exemplar (sweep $0.80/0.82/0.84$). \textbf{Detector construction and
circularity.} The lexicon, exemplars, and threshold were hand-seeded during an
8-prompt pilot on OLMo-2-1B and frozen \emph{before} the present 140-prompt
battery was authored; all three control conditions and 100 of the 140 prompts
were written after freezing. The sample sizes refer to distinct datasets: the
140 prompts form the behavioral evaluation, whereas 120 stratified final-model
sentences form a detector-accuracy audit. We audit those sentences
(40/30/20/20/10 across the five conditions) using two complete, independent,
blinded AI passes and Claude Opus 4.8 adjudication of their three disagreements.
This is an AI diagnostic, not human validation. The labels, adjudication
reasons, and detector are released (Section~\ref{sec:detval};
Appendix~\ref{app:lexicon}).

\subsection{Prompt battery and generation}
\label{sec:method-battery}
Five conditions isolate the axes the ``advice template'' conflates:
\textbf{advice} (60 personal/emotional help requests), \textbf{factual} (30
knowledge questions), \textbf{emotional non-advice} (20 descriptive prompts
about emotional topics, no help request), \textbf{neutral advice} (20 how-to
requests with no emotional stakes), and \textbf{domain factual} (10 impersonal
clinical-information questions). Each prompt is answered with \textbf{five
sampled generations} (temperature $0.7$, top-$p$ $0.9$, 256 new tokens,
distinct seeds).

\subsection{Statistical unit}
\label{sec:method-stats}
The clustering unit is the \textbf{prompt}: each prompt contributes its mean
detector-positive indicator over five generations, and condition rates are means over
prompts. All between-condition inference uses \textbf{prompt-clustered
permutation tests} (10{,}000 permutations of prompt-level means; two-sided;
Monte Carlo $p=(b+1)/(B+1)$). Benjamini--Hochberg correction is applied
separately to the 24 matched-control, 28 adjacent-stage, and 8 DPO pairwise
families.
We do \emph{not} treat generations as independent Bernoulli trials:
Fisher/Wilson statistics computed on ``equivalent counts'' are reported only
as an appendix sensitivity analysis with that caveat
(Appendix~\ref{app:sensitivity}).

\subsection{Stagewise evaluation and stage-data search}
\label{sec:method-stagewise}
The same evaluation runs on every public checkpoint: 1B
Base$\to$SFT$\to$DPO$\to$RLVR1$\to$Instruct; 7B
Base$\to$SFT$\to$DPO$\to$Instruct. Base checkpoints have no chat template and
use a plain Question/Answer format (a comparison caveat we state). The
stagewise grid runs the 90 advice$+$factual prompts; all five conditions run on the final
models. For each stage's data we search: the exact Stage-1 corpus
(\texttt{v4\_olmo-mix-1124\_llama}; novelty $=1-$verbatim coverage), each
model's exact SFT mixture in DuckDB with \textbf{assistant-only} scoping
($866{,}138$ / $939{,}344$ conversations for 1B/7B; oasst1 not searched
separately since it is an internal subset), the DPO preference mixes
(pair-level chosen-only/rejected-only counts, exact McNemar tests, and phrase
occurrences per million response words), and the
RLVR sets. SFT recoverability compares behavioral phrases against
\textbf{length-matched topical controls} from the same answers, per-million
documents, with a distinct-phrase leave-one-out.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} Stagewise, condition, and corpus numbers derive
from \texttt{summary\_v3.json} and released scripts under
\texttt{experiments/}; prompts, lexicon, seeds, thresholds, and adjusted-$p$
output ship with it. Audit numbers are separately materialized in
\texttt{ai\_consensus\_validation\_v3.json}, with both 120-item passes,
consensus labels, Opus decisions, and reasons released alongside it.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{Results at a glance.} Four outcomes organize the analysis. First,
the apparent advice bundle decomposes into affect-linked empathy, form-linked
structuring, domain-linked referral, and a validation marker consistently
elevated on advice. Second, all eight advice-minus-factual detector gaps widen
at SFT (six survive correction), while DPO selectively widens expressive gaps
and spreads structuring to factual answers. Third, exact phrase searches find
referral and structuring enrichment in DPO chosen responses but almost none in
the searched RLVR data. Fourth, the separate 120-sentence audit finds low
detector precision, so the checkpoint results are marker trajectories rather
than estimates of semantic behavior prevalence.

\subsection{The detector-positive advice bundle decomposes at the primary threshold}
\label{sec:decomposition}

Table~\ref{tab:conditions} gives mean prompt-level detector-positive rates per condition on
the final models; Table~\ref{tab:contrasts} tests advice against each matched
control. Against \emph{plain factual} controls every detector category separates
sharply in both models (all prompt-clustered $p<10^{-4}$; referral $0.927$
vs $0.073$ at 1B, $0.913$ vs $0.060$ at 7B). But the matched controls show
this is \textbf{not one advice-specific template}:

\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{Empathy expression tracks emotional content at $\tau=.82$.} It
fires \emph{more} on emotional non-advice descriptions than on advice ($0.79$ vs
$0.587$ at 1B, $p=.005$; $0.76$ vs $0.617$ at 7B, $p=.098$), and near-zero on
neutral advice ($0.03$--$0.07$).
\item \textbf{Structuring language tracks the how-to form.} It is at
ceiling on \emph{neutral} advice exactly as on personal advice (diff $0.00$,
$p=1.0$ at 1B; $-0.03$, $p=.57$ at 7B).
\item \textbf{Professional referral tracks the advice/health domain at
$\tau=.82$.} It fires on
$0.66$--$0.72$ of neutral-advice and $0.80$--$0.82$ of clinical-information
prompts; its advice increment over domain-factual is small and not significant
($+0.127$, $p=.053$ at 1B; $+0.093$, $p=.159$ at 7B).
\item \textbf{The validation marker is the only detector category consistently elevated on
personal advice relative to every matched control}: higher on advice than
\emph{every} control in \emph{both} models (all unadjusted $p\leq.02$; all
BH-adjusted $q\leq.028$), including emotional non-advice ($0.36$ vs $0.16$, $p=.008$;
$0.383$ vs $0.13$, $p=.003$).
\end{itemize}

Within the frozen detector, the primary-threshold marker bundle separates into affect-linked empathy,
form-linked structuring, domain-linked referral, and a validation marker consistently
elevated on advice. These are not detector-invariant universals: the threshold
sweep preserves the structuring result but qualifies the empathy and referral
contrasts (Section~\ref{sec:detval}). The 1B-vs-7B differences are small and
mixed; we make no scaling claim.

\subsection{Stagewise marker dynamics: every gap widens at SFT}
\label{sec:stagewise}

Raw advice-side rates rising across stages would be ambiguous because post-training
could simply make \emph{all} outputs more cautious and listy. The right quantity
is the \textbf{advice-minus-factual gap} $\Delta_s$ at each stage
(Table~\ref{tab:stagewise}) and its \emph{change} across adjacent stages, a
stage$\times$condition difference-in-differences tested with a prompt-clustered
permutation test on per-prompt stage trajectories (Table~\ref{tab:interaction};
10k permutations, $n{=}60$ advice/$30$ factual). Three findings.

\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{SFT widens every gap.} All eight base$\to$SFT changes are positive
(raw $p<0.05$; referral $+0.33$/$+0.28$, structuring $+0.39$/$+0.23$). BH
correction across all 28 adjacent-stage$\times$behavior tests preserves six;
validation is marginal ($q=.087$/$.055$). Every detector category is already nonzero at
base, so SFT widens advice-minus-factual marker gaps for categories already
detected at Base.
\item \textbf{DPO acts marker-specifically.} It significantly further widens
the \emph{expressive-marker} gaps (validation $+0.16$/$+0.15$, $p\leq0.008$; 1B empathy
$+0.15$, $p=0.005$) but \emph{not} the referral gap ($+0.10$/$+0.08$,
$p=0.067$/$0.109$), which is already near ceiling on advice after SFT. Its
preference data nonetheless contains more referral phrasings in chosen than
rejected responses (Table~\ref{tab:dpo}),
consistent with \emph{maintaining} that near-saturated gap.
\item \textbf{DPO collapses the structuring gap.} Its gap falls
$-0.38$/$-0.28$ at DPO ($p<10^{-4}$), not because advice-side structuring drops
(it is at ceiling) but because DPO raises structuring on \emph{factual} answers
from $0.07$ to $0.54$--$0.59$. Preference tuning spreads structuring language
everywhere while leaving the other advice-minus-factual detector gaps positive.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{RLVR and the final merge move no gap significantly} (every
dpo$\to$rlvr and final-merge transition has $p\geq0.21$), consistent with RLVR's
math-only data (Section~\ref{sec:datalayers}). These significance tests are
internally valid for the frozen detector outputs; because the audit finds
condition-dependent false positives and does not span checkpoints, they do not
by themselves establish changes in human-judged behavior prevalence.

\subsection{The data layers provide compatible phrase-level evidence}
\label{sec:datalayers}

\paragraph{SFT: a finite, auditable referral cluster (phrase-sparse).}
Assistant-only search of each model's exact mixture surfaces thousands of
referral demonstrations: ``healthcare professional'' ($3075$/$3106$ for
1B/7B), ``mental health professional'' ($2109$/$2120$), ``professional help''
($1765$/$1774$), ``seek professional'' ($1353$/$1355$). Length-matched
recoverability is above topical controls at $\geq 3$ words (3-word:
$19.5\times$ at 1B, $7.3\times$ at 7B; 4-word: $141.7\times$/$14.5\times$) but
\textbf{phrase-sparse}: only 5/6 distinct 3-word types, dominated by the list
preamble ``here are some.'' The distinct-phrase leave-one-out is the honest
figure: dropping that one phrase lowers the 3-word mean from $3358.1$ to
$\mathbf{758.8}$/M (1B) and $3026.0$ to $\mathbf{1065.9}$/M (7B)
(Appendix~\ref{app:perphrase}). Any inference must use the 5--6 distinct types
as its unit, not the $46$/$55$ occurrences. Role-scoping remains necessary:
blob search inflates ``talk to a'' $1.41$--$1.43\times$ via user turns.

\paragraph{DPO: paired and length-normalized enrichment.}
The exact mixes contain $378{,}301$/$378{,}341$ preference pairs. Chosen
responses are slightly \emph{shorter} in aggregate (chosen/rejected mean-word
ratio $0.990$/$0.997$), ruling out a general length explanation. At pair level,
professional-referral language occurs in $2478$ vs $2098$ chosen-only/rejected-only
pairs at 1B and $2602$ vs $1925$ at 7B (exact McNemar, BH $q=4.1\times10^{-8}$ /
$2.1\times10^{-23}$); per-million-word enrichment remains $1.12\times$/$1.25\times$.
Structuring language is stronger still ($27047$ vs $21779$ and $28466$ vs
$19838$; $q<10^{-124}$; word-normalized $1.16\times$/$1.27\times$). Empathy is
weakly enriched only at 1B after correction, and validation is not significant
(Table~\ref{tab:dpo}). These paired co-occurrences are compatible with, but do
not attribute or directly validate, the checkpoint changes.

\paragraph{RLVR: a clean negative.} The RLVR datasets (GSM/MATH/IF) contain
essentially none of the advice phrasings: every referral, empathy, and
validation phrase counts $0$--$1$ across \texttt{RLVR-GSM},
\texttt{RLVR-MATH}, and the IF-constraints set (only generic markers appear:
``first,'' $881$, ``here are some'' $48$ in the IF set). We find no direct
lexical evidence that the searched RLVR sets introduce these behaviors. Their
stability through the RLVR stage (Table~\ref{tab:stagewise}) is consistent with
retention rather than new introduction at RLVR.

\paragraph{Pretraining: not a verbatim echo (secondary, hedged).} Against the
exact Stage-1 corpus, advice answers are more novel than factual ones
($0.559$ vs $0.383$ at 1B; $0.525$ vs $0.379$ at 7B). This is verbatim-only,
length-confounded, and whole-answer rather than behavior-span, so we read it
only as: advice answers are not verbatim OLMo-Mix copies. The base
checkpoints' nonzero behavior rates are the stronger evidence that the
\emph{capability} predates post-training.

\subsection{Detector audit and threshold sensitivity}
\label{sec:detval}

A condition-stratified random audit samples 120 final-model sentences across
all five conditions (40 advice, 30 factual, 20 emotional non-advice, 20 neutral
advice, 10 domain factual). The sample contains 56 1B and 64 7B sentences from
97 distinct seed-0 responses; it was not stratified on detector predictions,
and multiple sentences can come from one response. Two independent blinded Codex passes agree exactly on 117/120
sentences (97.5\%); category agreement is 99.2--100\%, with Cohen's $\kappa$
$=.885/.663/1.000/.978$ for empathy/validation/referral/structuring. Claude
Opus 4.8 adjudicates the three disagreements. This establishes reproducible AI
consensus, not human ground truth.

Against that consensus, the detector has \textbf{low precision and
category-dependent recall} (Table~\ref{tab:detval}; four-category macro P
$0.28$, R $0.72$, F1 $0.36$; empathy and validation recall are each $0.50$).
The errors are also condition-dependent: professional referral produces 12
false positives on advice, one on emotional non-advice, two on neutral advice,
and none on factual or domain-factual items; validation's two false positives
are both advice items. Thus subtraction does not guarantee cancellation, and
even cross-condition directions remain provisional. \texttt{crisis\_referral}
again fails (0 TP / 11 FP) and is excluded. Absolute output rates are therefore
upward-biased (not formal upper bounds because recall is below one), while the
stagewise results should be read as trajectories of a frozen marker detector,
not estimates of semantic behavior prevalence.

The low positive prevalence yields wide Wilson 95\% intervals (e.g., referral
precision $[.011,.283]$ and validation precision $[.061,.792]$; all intervals
ship with the artifact), and the sentence-level sample does not directly
validate answer-level emission. The audit itself is AI-only. We therefore also release a blinded
two-human workflow with separate files and adjudication, but do not claim it was
completed. The threshold sweep is informative rather than uniformly reassuring
(Appendix~\ref{app:threshold}): structuring's advice--neutral contrast stays
within $.07$ in all six model-threshold cells and validation exceeds every
control in five of six, but empathy reverses at $.84$ and referral changes
magnitude. These are robustness checks on the detector, not a validated
taxonomy.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{From ``advice template'' to trained marker trajectories.} The
detector-positive decomposition and stagewise gaps nominate a testable account:
relevant language is already detected at Base; SFT widens every
advice-minus-factual marker gap (six of eight changes survive correction); DPO then acts
selectively by significantly widening the expressive gaps and collapsing the
structuring gap (spreading organizers to factual answers) while leaving the
already-saturated referral gap unmoved, even as referral phrasings show strong
paired, word-normalized chosen-response enrichment; no RLVR-stage gap change is
detected. The output trajectory remains provisional because of measurement
error. The strongest evidence is instead phrase-level and independently
auditable: the DPO chosen/rejected differential and RLVR zero-counts do not rely
on the embedding threshold, although they still establish co-occurrence rather
than causation.

\paragraph{Safety implications.} Professional-referral phrasing, the
bundle's safety-relevant marker, localizes to a finite, inspectable SFT
demonstration cluster and measurable DPO chosen-response enrichment. That makes the marker
auditable, and also fragile: mixture or preference-annotation changes could
silently weaken it. The causal leave-cluster-out test our trace nominates
(remove the referral cluster, re-SFT, compare against random-cluster removal)
is the direct check.

\paragraph{Methodological lessons.} (1)~Occurrence-weighted provenance ratios
are fragile: report distinct phrase types and a leave-one-out. (2)~Advice-side
rates across stages are ambiguous; report stage$\times$condition \emph{gaps}.
(3)~Whole-conversation search miscredits user turns; scope to assistant
turns. (4)~Cluster inference at the prompt, not the sample.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{Observational throughout.} Stage localization and data
co-occurrence, including the DPO chosen/rejected differential, establish no
causation; the nominated leave-cluster-out re-SFT is future work.
\item \textbf{Detector and threshold.} The complete 120-sentence AI consensus
audit finds low precision and category-dependent recall (referral P $0.06$;
empathy and validation R $0.50$) plus
condition-dependent errors. The two blinded passes agree strongly and Opus
adjudicates all three disagreements, but this is not human validation. Rates
are upward-biased; absolute levels, cross-condition gaps, and checkpoint
trajectories remain provisional. Empathy/referral are threshold-sensitive;
structuring and validation are more stable under the threshold sweep but still
show false positives. A provenance-safe two-human workflow is released.
\item \textbf{Coverage gaps.} Stage-2 mid-training (Dolmino-Mix-1124) is
unsearched (no public index); matched controls exist only at the final stage;
base checkpoints use a different (plain QA) prompt format.
\item \textbf{Phrase sparsity.} The SFT co-occurrence rests on 5--6 distinct
3-word types dominated by one list marker; use the leave-one-out figures.
\item \textbf{Statistical scope.} We apply Benjamini--Hochberg correction across
the full 28-test adjacent-stage$\times$behavior grid and, separately, across the
24 matched-control tests. Six of eight Base$\to$SFT changes survive; the two
validation changes are marginal. Five seeds bound but do not eliminate sampling
variance; prompt counts are modest; one model family, two sizes, one decoding
configuration; no scaling claim.
\item \textbf{Novelty} is verbatim-only, length-confounded, and whole-answer.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:roadmap}

Machine-advice language in OLMo-2 is not one indivisible template. A frozen
detector yields distinct affect-, form-, and domain-linked trajectories: all
eight advice--factual marker gaps widen at SFT (six survive multiplicity
correction), while DPO widens expressive-marker gaps and spreads structuring
markers to factual answers. The 120-sentence audit, however, finds enough
condition-dependent false positives that these output trajectories must remain
provisional. The strongest result is detector-independent at the matching
stage: exact professional-referral and structuring phrases are enriched in DPO
chosen responses under paired tests and word normalization, while the searched
RLVR data contain almost none. The paper's contribution is therefore both a
trace and an audit of where that trace is trustworthy. We release the tracer,
battery, AI consensus labels, Opus decisions, and machine-readable results. The
nominated next steps are causal and measurement-side:
leave-cluster-out re-SFT against random-cluster controls
\citep[cf.][]{xiao2026probe}, completion of the stratified two-annotator
validation, and a self-hosted Stage-2 index.

\section*{Ethics Statement}

\paragraph{Data and human subjects.} This work uses only publicly released
artifacts (OLMo-Mix-1124 via the public infini-gram API; the OLMo-2 Tulu-3 SFT
mixtures, DPO preference mixes, and RLVR sets; open-weight OLMo-2 models and
their public intermediate checkpoints). We collected no new data from people.
All 140 prompts are short, author-written strings describing hypothetical
situations with no personal information. The study involves no human subjects
and needed no ethics-board review.

\paragraph{Sensitive domain.} Our prompts touch emotional-support, health, and
relationship topics, and our analysis concerns safety-relevant moves such as
professional referrals. Given the detector's low precision we report detector-positive
rates as upward-biased and drop \texttt{crisis\_referral}. Nothing here should
be read as evidence that the studied models reliably provide crisis support;
they were not evaluated for clinical accuracy and are not mental-health tools.

\paragraph{Dual use.} Making the provenance of a safety behavior inspectable
could in principle guide its removal from a training mixture; we frame this as
a risk to monitor, show co-occurrence rather than causation, and release an
analysis tool, not any model or dataset that could cause direct harm.

\section*{Use of AI Assistants}

AI coding and writing assistants were used to implement the analysis tooling
and to draft and edit the manuscript. Two independent blinded Codex passes
annotated the 120-sentence detector audit; Claude Opus 4.8 adjudicated their
three disagreements. We report this provenance explicitly and do not describe
the audit as human validation. The author designed the study, chose the
methodology and metrics, and made the scientific decisions, including dropping
\texttt{crisis\_referral} and choosing the statistical unit. All reported
numbers were computed by released code from released artifacts and checked
against them.

\section{Behavior lexicon and detector threshold}
\label{app:lexicon}

A category fires for an answer if \emph{either} (i)~any lexicon phrase occurs
as a case-insensitive substring, \emph{or} (ii)~any sentence has cosine
similarity $\geq\tau$ to a seed exemplar
(\texttt{intfloat/multilingual-e5-small}, \texttt{query:}/\texttt{passage:}
prefixes), $\tau=0.82$ primary.

\paragraph{Lexicon (substring match).}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
\item \textbf{empathy expression} (\texttt{empathy\_opener}): \texttt{i'm sorry to hear}, \texttt{i am sorry to hear}, \texttt{i'm really sorry}, \texttt{that sounds really}, \texttt{that sounds very}, \texttt{i can understand}, \texttt{i can imagine}, \texttt{it's understandable}, \texttt{that must be}, \texttt{i'm here for you}, \texttt{i hear you}, \texttt{it sounds like you}

\item \textbf{professional referral} (\texttt{disclaimer}): \texttt{consult a}, \texttt{talk to a}, \texttt{speak with a}, \texttt{see a doctor}, \texttt{seek medical}, \texttt{i am not a doctor}, \texttt{i'm not a doctor}, \texttt{medical professional}, \texttt{healthcare professional}, \texttt{mental health professional}, \texttt{seek professional}, \texttt{professional help}

\item \textbf{structuring language} (\texttt{structure}): \texttt{here are some}, \texttt{here are a few}, \texttt{consider the following}, \texttt{steps you can take}, \texttt{a few things you can}, \texttt{first,}, \texttt{secondly,}

about this.'' Structuring: ``Here are a few steps you can take'';

\section{Statistical formulas}
\label{app:formulas}

Primary inference uses two-sided prompt-clustered permutation tests on
prompt-level means (10{,}000 draws), with Monte Carlo
$p=(b+1)/(B+1)$; $p<10^{-4}$ denotes zero raw exceedances.
Appendix~\ref{app:sensitivity} reports Wilson 95\% intervals, two-sided Fisher
tests, and Haldane--Anscombe odds ratios on equivalent counts. The
distinct-phrase leave-one-out removes the most frequent 3-word phrase before
recomputing the mean behavioral per-million. DPO comparisons use exact McNemar
tests on chosen-only versus rejected-only pairs and phrase occurrences per
million whitespace-delimited response words. Released scripts derive all
statistics from the artifact.
